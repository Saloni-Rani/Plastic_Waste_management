At a glance

Global plastic production has doubled since 2000, reaching about 460 million tonnes in 2019. In the same year, the world generated 353 million tonnes of plastic waste. After accounting for losses in processing, only about 9% of plastic waste was recycled, roughly 19% incinerated, nearly 50% landfilled, and around 22% mismanaged (dumped, openly burned, or leaked to the environment). Source: OECD Global Plastics Outlook

UNEP estimates that 19–23 million tonnes of plastic leak into lakes, rivers, and seas every year, and without additional action, emissions of plastic into aquatic ecosystems could nearly triple by 2040. Source: UNEP

Organic Waste

Waste is a major environmental issue that affects the quality of our surroundings, health, and the natural ecosystems. Waste can be classified into two primary types: organic and inorganic waste.

Inorganic Waste

inorganic waste originates from chemical substances and materials that do not come from living beings, such as glass, plastic, and metal. The decomposition of inorganic waste is a slow process that can take thousands of years, making their recycling more complex.

Recyclable Waste

The Waste which can be Recycled is called Recyclable Waste.

Radioactive Waste

Radioactive waste is broadly classified into low-level waste (LLW), such as paper, rags, tools, clothing, which contain small amounts of mostly short-lived radioactivity, intermediate-level waste (ILW), which contains higher amounts of radioactivity and requires some shielding.

preventive Measures

preventive Measures

Reduce and Reuse:

Encourage industries to minimize the use of single-use plastics and adopt reusable and sustainable alternatives.

Recycling:

Implement and promote effective recycling programs for industrial plastic waste to reduce the amount of plastic entering landfills and ecosystems.

Circular Economy :

Embrace a circular economy approach, where the lifecycle of plastic products is optimized through recycling, reusing, and reducing waste.

Regulations and Policies:

Implement and enforce strict regulations on the disposal and management of industrial plastic waste to ensure responsible practices.

Innovation and Research:

Invest in research and innovation to develop eco-friendly alternatives to traditional plastics and more sustainable industrial processes.

Where does plastic waste come from?

  • Short‑lived products dominate: nearly two‑thirds of plastic waste comes from items with lifetimes under five years.
  • Packaging is the largest share (~40%).
  • Consumer goods (~12%) and clothing & textiles (~11%) are other major sources.
Source: OECD press release summarizing Global Plastics Outlook
Production and Consumption
  • Production and Consumption: Raw Material Use: The production of plastic involves the extraction and processing of fossil fuels, such as oil and natural gas. This process contributes to environmental degradation and resource depletion.
  • Single-Use Plastics: Industries often use plastic for packaging, leading to a large amount of single-use plastic waste. This contributes significantly to the plastic pollution problem.
Waste Generation
  • Manufacturing Waste: During the manufacturing of plastic products, waste is generated in the form of defective or excess materials.
  • End-of-life Products: When plastic products reach the end of their life cycle, they often become waste, either through disposal or improper management.
Recycling Challenges
  • Complexity of Plastic Recycling: Many plastics are challenging to recycle due to variations in composition, color, and the presence of additives.
  • Limited Recycling Infrastructure: Inadequate recycling infrastructure in many regions hinders the efficient collection and processing of plastic waste.
Pollution and Environmental Impact
  • Land and Water Pollution: Improper disposal of plastic waste leads to pollution of land and water bodies. Plastics can take hundreds of years to degrade, causing long-term environmental damage.
  • Microplastics: Over time, larger plastic items break down into smaller particles known as microplastics, which can enter ecosystems and potentially harm aquatic life.
Health Concerns
  • Toxic Additives: Some plastics contain additives that can be harmful to human health. When plastic waste degrades or incinerates, these additives may release toxins into the environment.
  • Food Chain Contamination: Plastics can enter the food chain when consumed by animals, potentially leading to human exposure to harmful substances.
Regulatory Measures
  • Legislation and Policies: Governments and international organizations are implementing regulations to reduce single-use plastics, promote recycling, and encourage the responsible management of plastic waste.
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): Some regions have adopted EPR programs, requiring producers to take responsibility for the end-of-life management of their products, including proper disposal and recycling.
Innovations and Solutions
  • Biodegradable Plastics: The development of biodegradable plastics aims to reduce the environmental impact of plastic waste.
  • Circular Economy Approaches: Implementing circular economy principles, such as designing for recyclability and promoting the reuse of plastics, can help minimize waste generation.
